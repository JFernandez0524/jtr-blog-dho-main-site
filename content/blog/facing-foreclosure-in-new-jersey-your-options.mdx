## How Foreclosure Works in New Jersey

The first thing to understand is that foreclosure is a legal process, and it takes time.

It usually starts with a **Notice of Intent to Foreclose**, which your lender is required to send before filing anything with the court. After that, the next step is typically a **lis pendens** — a legal filing that officially begins the court process. If you've received one of these, here's [what a lis pendens means and what to expect](/blog/what-is-a-lis-pendens-in-new-jersey).

New Jersey is a judicial foreclosure state, so everything goes through the courts. That process has multiple stages, and at each one, homeowners still have opportunities to take action. For a full breakdown of what happens at each stage, take a look at the [New Jersey foreclosure timeline](/blog/foreclosure-timeline-new-jersey).

## Options You May Still Have

Even if foreclosure proceedings have started, many homeowners still have paths forward. Here are the most common ones:

**Loan Modification or Repayment Plan**
You may be able to work directly with your lender to restructure your loan or set up a plan to catch up on missed payments. Lenders often prefer this over going through a lengthy foreclosure.

**Selling the Property**
If keeping the home isn't realistic, selling before the foreclosure progresses further can protect the equity you've built. A sale on your terms is almost always a better outcome than waiting for the bank to take the property.

**Short Sale**
If you owe more than the home is worth, a short sale may be an option. This is where the lender agrees to accept less than the full mortgage balance. It's not ideal, but it typically does far less damage to your credit than a foreclosure. [Here's how short sales compare to foreclosure](/blog/short-sale-vs-foreclosure).

## Why Acting Early Matters

Every situation is different, and the best path forward depends on your financial situation, your goals, and the condition of the property.

But one thing is true across the board: **waiting too long makes things harder.** The longer you wait, the more fees and interest pile up — and the more your equity shrinks. Acting early gives you the most options and the most control over the outcome.
